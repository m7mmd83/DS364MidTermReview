What Digital Curation Is:

  • The process of managing and preserving digital information over time
  • It involves collecting, organizing, describing, preserving, and providing access to digital informaiton over time
  • It deals with issues related to data protection and risk management to ensure that data is available now and in the future
  • It is important because it ensures digital information is available and accessible to future generations.

What Digital Curation Is NOT:

  • It is not digital archiving: "The process of backup and ongoing maintenance" as opposed to building strategies for long-term preservation
  • It is not digital preservation: "All of the actions required to maintain access to digital information beyond the limits of medial failure of technology change"

  • Encouraging data sharing and reuse

Open Archival Information System (OAIS):

  • An archive, consisting of an organization of people and systems, that has accepted the responsibility to preserve information and make it available for a designated community.

The Key Functions of an OAIS are:

  1. Ingest
  2. Archival Storage
  3. Data Management
  4. Administration
  5. Access
  6. Preservation Planning
  7. Common Services

Actors Are:

Producers:
Individuals, organizations, or computer systems that transfer data to the OAIS for preservation.
Managers:
Develop policies, define scope, and perfrom other management functions.
Consumers
Individuals, organizations, or systems that are expected to use the digital information preserved by the OAIS.

Objects in OAIS are:

  1. Submission Information Package SIP
  2. Archival Information Package AIP
  3. Dissemination Information Package DIP

An information package has three parts:

  1. The digital object to be preserved.
  2. The metadata required at that point in the system.
  3. Packaging information.

The Submission Information Package SIP:

  • It is what arrives at the repository.
  • It consists of the digital object, any descriptive or technical metadata, and any other information deemed relevant by the content provider.
  • SIPs my be provided to an OAIS from another repository.

The Archival Information Package AIP:

  • It is produced by taking the SIP and adding to it further informatoin about the digital object, if required.
  • The added information is either Preservation Description Information or Representation Information.

Preservation Description Information:

  • Needed to manage preservation of the digital objects.
  • It has four parts:
    1. Reference Information.
    2. Provenance Information.
    3. Context Information.
    4. Fixity Information.

Representation Information:

  • Required to make the content of the digital object intelligible to its designate community.
  • It is the technical metadata required to make the bit stream retrievable as a meaningful digital object.

The Dissemination Information Package DIP:

  • Produced when a user requests access to a digital object in the OAIS.
  • It consists of the content data plus any metadata and support systems necessary to retrieve and use the content data
  • The nature of the metadata and the Representation Information is determined by the assumed knowledge of the designated community

Data refers to:

  • Any information in binary digital form

Digital objects can be simple or complex:

Simple Digital Objects:
Discrete digital items, such as images or textual files, along with their related identifiers and metadata.
Complex Digital Objects:
Discrete digital objects, made by combining a number of other digital objects, like Web sites.

Databases are:

  • Structured collections of records or data stored in a computer system.

Databases are controlled by database management software (DBMS)

Born-Digital & Digitized Data:

Born-digital materials:
Materials created using a computer and therefore existing in a digital version
Digitized materials:
The result of a process of digitizing analog materials.

The distinction between them is not important for digital curation practices.

Metadata is defined as:

  • Summarize data content, context, structure, interrelationships, and provenance.
  • They add relevance and purpose to data, and enable identification of similar data in different data collections.

Metadata is data too!

Module 03

Digital Curation Aims to retain 3 Characteristics:

1. Longevity:
The availability of data for as long as their current and future users require them.
2. Integrity:
Refers to the authenticity of the data. That they have not been manipulated, forged, or substituted.
3. Accessability:
Requires that we can locate and use data in the future in a way that is acceptable to their designated community.

Scope of Digital Curation:

A. Ensuring Longevity:
  1. Refreshing data.
  2. Checking accuracy of the results of refreshing.
  3. Generate metadata that document the process applied to refreshing data.
  4. Maintaining multiple copies of the bit stream.
  5. Keeping track of changes in hardware, software, file formats and standards that might have an impact on digital preservation.
B. Ensuring Integrity:
  1. Refreshing data.
  2. Checking accuracy of the results of refreshing.
  3. Generate metadata that document the processes applied to refreshing data.
  4. Protecting data by managing them in accordance to good IT practices for data security, backups, and error checking.
  5. Maintaining multiple copies of the bit stream.
  6. Managing intellectual property and other rights.
C. Maintaining Accessibility:
  1. Assigning persistent identifiers to locate digital materials.
  2. Recoring sufficient representation information so that the bit stream is still meaningful and understandable in the future.
  3. Producing digital objects in open, well-supported standard formats.
  4. Limiting the range of preservation formats to be managed.
  5. Maintaining multiple copies of the bit stream.
  6. Keeping track of changes in hardware, software, file formats, and standards that might have an impact on digital preservation.

Some Practical & Technical Curation Tasks for Scientists & Research Groups are:

  1. Applying open source software and open standards.
  2. Creating metadata and annotations
  3. Linking related research materials
  4. Using persistent identifiers.
  5. Being consistent about citation formats.
  6. Deciding which digital objects to curate over the longer term.
  7. Keeping storage devices current.
  8. Validating and authenticating migrated data.

Types of Metadata

1. Administrative metadata:
Related to the use, management, and encoding of digital objects over time. Includes subsets for technical metadata, rights management metadata, and preservation metadata.
2. Descriptive metadata:
Decribes work for the purpose of discovery and identification, such as creator, title, and subject.
3. Technical metadata:
A form of administrative metadata dealing with the creation or storage encoding process or format of the resource.
4. Structural metadata:
Indicates how compound objects are structured.
5. Preservation metadata:
Administrative metadata dealing with provenance and archival management.

Key Activities For Description and Representation Information Action are:

  • Appreciating the need to Description and Representation Information.
  • Being aware where they are required.
  • Understanding the key standards that exist for them.
  • Developing policies for applying them.

Representation Information in OAIS is Divided Into Three Classes:

1. Structure Information.
Describes the format and digital structures for processing and rendering digital object. Information about file formats.
2. Semantic Information.
Information about the content of the digital object that defines the relationships among the objects or parts of the objects.
3. Other Representation Information.
Necessary information to interpret the digital object. Information about relevant software, hardware, or storage media; encryption or compression algorithms; or documentation.

Metadata Standards Address Three Main Areas:

  • The structure notes that elements to be applied.
  • The smentics describe the meanings of the elements selected.
  • The syntax determines how elements are written.

The most common metadata standards applied to digital curation are Preservation Metadata:

  1. Implementation Strategies PREMIS
  2. Metadata Encoding & Transmission Standards METS
  3. Metadata Object Description Schema MODS
  4. Metadata Authority Description Schema MADS

These standards are all related to XML in different ways.

PREMIS

  • The international standard for preservation metadata.
  • The PREMIS Data Dictionary defines a core set of preservation metadata elements that has wide applicability in the preservation community.

The PREMIS data model defines relationsships between digital preservation "entities":

1. intellectual entities:
Not defined in PREMIS. Users should apply other metadata standards.
2. Objects:
Three types: representation, file, and bit stream.
3. Events:
Actions on an object in a preservation repository. They document provenance and track the history of the object.
4. Agents:
People, organizations, or software associated with preservation events in the life of an object.
5. Rights:
An agreement with a rights-holder that allows a repository to take actions in relations to objects in the repository.

METS

  • The standard for encodign in XML the metadata describing or characterizing digital objects.

A METS XML document has 5 major sections:

1. Descriptive metadata:
Contains descriptive metadata or pointers to external descriptive metadata, or both.
2. Administrative metadata:
Information about:
  1. How the files were created and stored.
  2. Intellectual property rights.
  3. The original source objec.
  4. The provenance of the files comprising the digital object.
3. File groups:
List all files comprising all versions of the digital object.
4. Structural map:
Outlines hierarchical structure of the digital object.
5. Behavior:
Associate executable behaviors with the content in the METS object.

Module 04

Key Activities encompassed by Preservation Planning are:

  • Appreciating the need to planning at all stages of curation.
  • Developing plans for all stages of curation.
  • periodically reviewing and updating curation procedures.

Risk Management as the Context for Preservation Planning:

  • Proactive preservation activities aimed at minimizing risks.
  • Active management of risks overtime is essential to presrve digital objects and ensure they remain usable in the future.
  • A risk management approach is increasingly common in the preservation of digital and non-digital materials.
  • Risk management is aimed at reducing the liklihood of compromising events and limiting their impact when they do.

Broad principles of risk management include protecting data by:

  • Regular backup procedure.
  • Maintaining multiple copies of the bit streams.
  • Having disaster recovery contingencies in place.
  • Providing stable and secure media storage conditions.
  • Copying data to more stable media at defined intervals.
  • Ensuring data security by implementing procedures for virus protection and unauthorized access.

Policy for Curation:

  • Developing policies for all aspects of digital curation is vital for its effectiveness.
  • Policies provide clear, long-term direction and guidance, and are regularly reviewed and updated.
  • They clearly state principles, values, and intentions.
  • They help an organization develop a digital curation strategy and plan coherent digital curation programs.

Good policies have elements in common:

  • They state what is, and what is not, allowed.
  • They indicate how the policy will be monitored and who is responsible for this.
  • They provide links to other relevant policies and statements about procedures.
  • They note the date when they are to be reviewed and how frequently.

The OAIS model suggests policies are required for:

  • Archival sotrage. (e.g., managing migration)
  • Management. (e.g., resource utilization, pricing)
  • Data recovery, and security. (e.g., physical access control)

Cost of Curation:

  • Planning for curation requires estimating the required resources.
  • Planning can be difficult because there has been little clarity about curation costs.
  • Compression makes a difference in storage costs for image files.
  • Other factors are: labor cost, hardware & software maintenance, media replacement, capital equipment, software licenses, and electricity.
  • Frequency of data migrations affect cost because they involve people despite increased automation.
  • Changing storage medium and the shift to cloud storage are also cost factors.

Key Activities encompassed by Community Watch and Participation are:

  • Keeping up to date with digital curation activities and developments in related areas.
  • Sharing data and participating in other activities that underpin data reuse.
  • Participating in the development of standards for digital curation.
  • Participating in the creation of tools and toolkits for digital curation.

Community Watch refers to:

  • Staying fully aware of other activities in the digital curation community on an ongoing basis.

Participation refers to:

  • The need for collaboration in the digital curation community.

The benefits of collaboration to digital curation are:

  • Sharing expertise.
  • Sharing cost of developing software and systems.
  • Sharing tools and systems of other organizations, and learning opportunities.
  • Encouraging influential stakeholders to take digital curation seriously.
  • Increased ability to influence data producers and system developers.
  • Joint research and development of standards and practices.
  • Enhanced ability to attract resources.

Standards: Essential for Digital Curation

  • Effective digital curation required the development and implementation of standards.
  • A fundamental aspect of digital curation is the sharing and resuing of data.
  • This implies the interoperability of systems "The ability of software and hardware to exchange and use information"
  • For reliable and consistent interoperability, standards are essential.

Key Activities encompassed by Create or Receive are:

  • Develop, document, and apply policies about creating and receiving data.
  • Infulence data creators to create curation-friendly data.
  • Create data in standard formats and file types that can be processed with open-source, well-documented programs.
  • Collect and keep documentation about data, formats, software, use agreements, and provenance.
  • Develop and implement procedures for receiving data.

Policies for Creating/Receiving Data Are Required to Establish:

  • Who is eligible to deposit data in the archive.
  • The data quality requirements.
  • which metadata must be submitted.
  • confidentiality and disclosure.
  • The access status of the data.
  • Data file formats.
  • Volume & size limitations.

Topics & Questions that policies for creating & receiving data should address are:

1. Eligible Depositors:
  • Who is eligible to deposit data?
  • What kind of data will be received?
  • What are the procedures for providing receipts to depositors?
2. Data Quality Requirements:
  • What quality criteria must the data meet?
  • Is the coverage complete?
  • Have they been checked for validy?
3. Metadata:
  • What metadata should be supplied by the creator?
  • What happens if this required metadata is not supplied?
  • What metadata will the archive supply?
4. Confientiality and disclosure:
  • What requirements must data creators meet regarding confidential data?
  • Does the data identify people?
  • Will the archive anonymize data?
5. Embargo Status:
  • Will an embargo be placed on the data?
  • How long will it last?
  • What are the triggers to lift it?
6. Rights & Ownership:
  • What rights are retained by the creator?
  • What rights are transferred to the archive?
  • What limitations are placed on how the archive can use the data?
  • Has the creator obtained permission from rights-owners?
  • Does the creator certify that the data do not infringe the copyright of others?
7. Data file formats:
  • Which formats are accepted for deposit?
  • Which formats are preferred?
  • Will the formats be normalized?
  • Will compression formats be accepted?
  • Will the archive retain the original bit stream as well?
  • Is there a limit on the size and number of files accepted?

To Keep data and to keep the ability to process it, it must be:

  • Authentic.
  • Accurate.
  • Renderable.
  • In a form that best ensures its longevity.

Criteria to predict the ongoing viability of a file format include:

1. Openness:
  • Is there an open specification for the format?
  • Are its specifications puplic domain?
  • Is it encrypted?
2. Portability:
  • Is the format independent of a specific operating system, software, or hardware?
  • Is it independent of particular organization or group?
  • Is it in widespread and current use?
  • Does it contain little or no built-in functionality?
3. Quality:
  • Is it robust, simple, tested, and loss-free?

The main Key Characteristics of Open-file formats:

  • Based on freely available standards.
  • Developed by a community.
  • Can be used in multiple software packages.
  • Do not contain intellectual property restrictions.

Documentation of digital objects is best carried out when they are created and should continue to be an ongoing part of the curation process.

Three Characteristics of file formats helpful for managing data are:

1. Metadata Support:
  1. Description and Representation Information are essential for curation
  2. Some software generate description & representation information automatically.
  3. Some file formats accommodate metadata.
2. Interoperability:
  1. Managing data over time require migration to another technical environment.
  2. Independent file formats are supported by a wide range of software and are easier to migrate.
3. Viability:
  1. Some file formats are stronger and can be still be accessed even if part of it is damaged.
  2. Some recommended file formats are not necessarily robust.
  3. Some file formats have built-in error checking mechanisms.
4. Data Quality:
  1. Data of high quality produces the best outcomes.
  2. Three critical points for data quality:
    • When data are collected.
    • When they are prepared for analysis.
    • When they are verified.

Structuring Data for Discoverability:

  • A presistent identifier does not change even if the location of the data changes.
  • Reliable identification is essential for long-term access.
  • Use of persistent identifiers is not limited to web material. It is essential for linking and citation of primary research to data sets.

Module 06

Key Activities encompassed by Appraise and Select are:

  • Developing, documenting, and applying policies about appraisla and selection, including
    1. Defining designated community.
    2. Identifying properties to preserve.
    3. Deciding how long data needs to be maintained.
  • Developing appraisal criteria.
  • Determining whether to keep data by evaluating them against appraisal criteria.

definition of Appraise and select:

  • The processes of developing and applying criteria for determining what data should be kept for the long term.
  • Appraisal is the process of deter the significance of any information object.
  • Selection is a more general term applied when deciding what materials will be added to a repository. Used in libary context.
  • Both rely on criteria for determining what is worthy for preservation and adding to a repository.

Appraisal & Selection policy addresses five themes:

  • Future users.
  • Feasibility of preservatoin.
  • Legat and intellectual rights.
  • Mission-criticality of data.
  • Associated data.

Information Professionals Rule in Appraisal & Select:

  • Responsible for developing selection policies and guidelines for appraisal.
  • Liaise with creators and depositors to ensure data sets are in the best share to ensure preservability.
  • Locate sufficient resources to ensure effective appraisal.

Data Creators Rule in appraisal & Select:

  • Ensure data sets have sufficient metadata and documentation.
  • Ensure data are in curation-ready formats
  • Have clear understanding of which data are vital, important, or minor.

The designated community is:

  • An identified group of potential consumers who should be able to understand a particular set of information.

Reappraise is an Occasional Action in the Curation Lifecycle Model.

The Key Tasks of Reappraisal are:

  • Specifying conditions that trigger a reappraisal.
  • Testing data sets that meet those conditions against appraisal criteria.

Dispose is an Occasional Action in the Curation Lifecycle Model

The Activities associated with Dispose are:

  • Dispose of data which has not been selected for long-term preservation according to documented policies, guidance, and legal requirements.
  • The options for disposing are:
    • Transferring data to another archive.
    • Destroying it in a secure manner.

Important Notes about Disposal of Data:

  • The decision of disposal is made by assessing data against appraisal criteria to determine whether they are relevant to their aims and are worthy for long-term preservation.
  • If a decision is made to dispose of it, data can either be transferred to another repository or destroyed.
  • The decision to dispose may arrise from reappraisal.
  • Data deemed irrelevant to a repository may be transferred to another one that is interested in them.
  • Appropriate adn adequate metadata and documentation about the data set must also be transferred.
  • Some data sets my need to be completely destroyed.
  • Legal requirements could mandate the destruction of data in a secure manner.

Module 07

Ingest is the Fourth Sequential Action in the DCC Curation Lifecycle Model.

Two sets of activities are given for Ingest:

  1. Transfer data to an archive.
  2. Adhere to documented guidance, policies, or legal requirements.

Ingest refers to the process of preparing data for adding to a digital archive and then adding them to it.

For the Ingest Action, Two kinds of Information package are important:

  1. Submission Information Package SIP
  2. Archival Information Package AIP

Preservation Description Information PDI has Four main components:

1. Reference Information:
  • A unique and persistent identifier.
2. Provenance Information:
  • The history of the archived object.
3. Context Information:
  • The relationship to other objects.
4. Fixity Information:
  • A demonstration of authenticity.

Main Steps in Receiving SIPs can be grouped into Four categories of actions:

1. Validation:
  • To ensure the received material i complete.
  • Identify characteristics that are needed for curation.
2. Health Check:
  • To ensure the quality of the data.
3. Annotation:
  • Related to the description and representation information associated with the data.
4. Transformation:
  • Applied based on the archive's policy on normalizing file formats.
  • Could be necessary to migrate data to a different file format as part of the ingest process.

Ingest Tools:

  • The cost of acquisition & ingest are high.
  • Many ingest procedures are labor intensive.
  • Automation is essential for handling larger quantities of data.
  • Automation is enabled by a range of tools developed for this purpose.
  • The COPTR tool registry shows a veriety of these toosl.

Policies for Ingest:

  • Well-developed policy statements and guidance allow for effective ingest process.
  • Policies must be documented and updated.
  • Policies clarify responsibilities, lines of communication, promote standardization, mange risk, and address compliance issues.
